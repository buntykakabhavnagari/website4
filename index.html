Q.1 Particle Size Determination Methods & Coulter Counter | Surface Area Methods | Powder Flow 16 Marks ▼
(a) Methods for Determination of Particle Size & Coulter Counter Method [06 Marks]

Methods for Determination of Particle Size:

  1. Microscopy Method (Optical & Electron) – Direct measurement of particle dimensions; useful for range 0.2–100 μm.
  2. Sieving Method – Uses standard sieves arranged in descending order; range 20–125,000 μm.
  3. Sedimentation Method (Andreasen Pipette) – Based on Stokes' law; range 1–200 μm.
  4. Coulter Counter Method – Electrical sensing zone principle; range 0.5–500 μm.
  5. Laser Diffraction Method – Based on Fraunhofer diffraction; range 0.1–3000 μm.
  6. Dynamic Light Scattering (Photon Correlation Spectroscopy) – For submicron particles; range 0.003–3 μm.
  7. Air Permeability Method – Gives specific surface area; indirect size measurement.

Coulter Counter Method – Detailed Explanation:

Principle: The Coulter counter works on the principle of electrical sensing zone (also called the resistive pulse sensing technique). When a particle suspended in an electrolyte solution passes through a small aperture (orifice), it displaces its own volume of electrolyte, causing a momentary change in electrical resistance. This change in resistance produces a voltage pulse whose magnitude is proportional to the volume of the particle.

Key Principle: The change in resistance (ΔR) is directly proportional to particle volume (V): ΔR ∝ V  →  Particle diameter can be calculated assuming spherical shape.
Construction & Working:
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ COULTER COUNTER │ │ │ │ ┌─────────┐ ┌─────────────┐ │ │ │ Glass │ Orifice │ Glass │ │ │ │ Chamber │ ●────●│ Chamber │ │ │ │ (Anode) │ (0.05- │ (Cathode) │ │ │ │ │ 2mm) │ │ │ │ │ + │ ↓ │ - │ │ │ │ └──────┘ ↓ └──────┬──────┘ │ │ │ ↓ │ │ │ │ Particle Amplifier │ │ │ Suspension │ │ │ │ │ ┌───┴───┐ │ │ └────────────┘ │ Size │ │ │ │Analyzer│ │ │ Mercury └───┬───┘ │ │ Manometer │ │ │ ┌───┴───┐ │ │ │Counter│ │ │ │/Plotter│ │ │ └───────┘ │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Components:

  • Two glass chambers: Separated by a glass tube with a precise circular orifice (aperture) of known diameter (typically 0.05–2 mm).
  • Electrolyte solution: Usually 0.9% NaCl or other conductive solution in which particles are suspended.
  • Electrodes: Platinum electrode in each chamber connected to a constant current source.
  • Mercury manometer: Controls the flow of suspension through the aperture by applying vacuum.
  • Amplifier & Pulse Analyzer: Amplifies the voltage pulse and converts pulse height to particle size.
  • Counter/Recorder: Counts and records number of particles in each size class.

Working Procedure:

  1. The electrolyte solution is placed in both chambers, and the system is checked for baseline stability.
  2. A well-dispersed sample of powder is added to the internal (anode) chamber and sonicated to break agglomerates.
  3. A vacuum is applied through the mercury manometer, causing suspension to flow through the orifice.
  4. As each particle passes through the aperture, it displaces electrolyte, increasing resistance momentarily.
  5. This produces a voltage pulse whose height is proportional to particle volume.
  6. Pulses are counted and classified by size using a multichannel analyzer.
  7. Size distribution is plotted as number vs. equivalent spherical diameter.

Equivalent Diameter: The Coulter counter measures the volume-equivalent diameter (dv):

dv = (6V / π)1/3

where V = particle volume.

Advantages:
  • Rapid, accurate, and highly reproducible
  • Number distribution obtained directly
  • Can analyze thousands of particles per second
  • Insensitive to particle optical properties
Limitations:
  • Requires particles suspended in electrolyte (not suitable for all materials)
  • Aperture can be blocked by large particles
  • Assumes spherical particles
  • Cannot distinguish between solid and hollow particles
💡 Exam Tip: Always draw the Coulter counter diagram with two chambers, orifice, electrodes, and mercury manometer. Mention that it gives volume-equivalent diameter and the principle is "displacement of electrolyte causing change in resistance."
(b) Methods for Determination of Particle Surface Area [05 Marks]

1. Air Permeability Method (Lea & Nurse Method / Fisher Sub-Sieve Sizer)

Principle: Based on the Kozeny-Carman equation. When air is passed through a packed bed of powder, the resistance to airflow depends on the specific surface area of the particles. Finer particles create more resistance (higher pressure drop) due to greater total surface area.

Kozeny-Carman Equation:

S0 = [14 × ε / (1 − ε)] × [√(ΔP × A × t / (η × L × m))]

Where:

  • S0 = Specific surface area (cm²/g)
  • ε = Porosity (void fraction) of the powder bed
  • ΔP = Pressure drop across the bed
  • A = Cross-sectional area of the bed
  • t = Time for air flow
  • η = Viscosity of air
  • L = Bed length (height)
  • m = Mass of powder

Procedure: Powder is packed into a cylindrical cell to a known porosity. Air is drawn through the bed at constant pressure, and the flow rate is measured using a manometer. The specific surface area is calculated using the Kozeny-Carman equation.

Fisher Sub-Sieve Sizer: A commercial instrument based on this principle; directly reads the average particle diameter from a calibrated chart after measuring pressure drop.

Important: This method gives surface-weighted (Sauter mean) diameter. Range: 0.2–50 μm. It is suitable for dry powders only.

2. BET Adsorption Method (Nitrogen Adsorption)

Principle: Based on the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) theory of multilayer gas adsorption. An inert gas (usually nitrogen) is adsorbed on the particle surface at liquid nitrogen temperature (−196°C). The amount of gas adsorbed at various pressures is measured, and from this, the total surface area is calculated.

BET Equation:

P / [V(P0 − P)] = (1 / VmC) + [(C − 1) / VmC] × (P / P0)

Where:

  • P = Equilibrium pressure of gas
  • P0 = Saturation pressure of gas at the adsorption temperature
  • V = Volume of gas adsorbed at pressure P
  • Vm = Volume of gas required to form a monolayer
  • C = BET constant related to heat of adsorption

Calculation of Surface Area:

SBET = (Vm × NA × σ) / M

Where:

  • NA = Avogadro's number (6.022 × 1023)
  • σ = Cross-sectional area of one adsorbate molecule (N2: 0.162 nm²)
  • M = Molar volume of gas at STP (22,414 cm³/mol)

Procedure: A known mass of powder is degassed under vacuum to remove adsorbed moisture/gases. The sample is cooled to liquid N2 temperature, and nitrogen gas is introduced incrementally. Adsorption isotherm is plotted (P/P0 vs. V), and BET equation is applied in the relative pressure range of 0.05–0.35.

📚 Short Revision Note: Air permeability = dry method, Kozeny-Carman equation, gives external surface area. BET adsorption = wet method, uses nitrogen gas at −196°C, gives total (internal + external) surface area. BET is more accurate for porous materials.
(c) Factors Affecting Powder Flow & Methods to Improve [05 Marks]

Factors Affecting Powder Flow:

A. Particle-Related Factors:

  1. Particle Size: Smaller particles have greater surface area, more interparticulate friction, and cohesive forces (van der Waals), leading to poor flow. Optimum size: 40–200 μm.
  2. Particle Size Distribution: Wide distribution improves flow (fine particles fill voids between coarse); narrow distribution leads to poor packing and flow.
  3. Particle Shape: Spherical particles flow best (least interlocking). Angular, needle-shaped, or plate-like particles have poor flow.
  4. Surface Texture: Smooth surfaces reduce friction → better flow. Rough surfaces increase mechanical interlocking → poor flow.
  5. Surface Moisture: Moisture acts as a bridge between particles, increasing cohesion and reducing flow.

B. Bulk-Related Factors:

  1. Porosity & Bulk Density: Higher bulk density (less porosity) generally indicates better flow.
  2. Particle Porosity: Highly porous particles are lighter and may flow poorly.

C. Environmental Factors:

  1. Humidity: High humidity increases moisture adsorption → poor flow.
  2. Temperature: Elevated temperature can cause softening, caking, or increased moisture effects.

D. Processing Factors:

  1. Consolidation/Storage Time: Prolonged storage under pressure increases cohesion (caking).
  2. Electrostatic Charge: Charged particles repel or attract, disrupting uniform flow.

Methods to Improve Powder Flow:

  1. Glidants: Addition of glidants such as colloidal silicon dioxide (Aerosil 200), talc, or magnesium stearate (0.1–2%) reduces interparticulate friction.
  2. Granulation: Wet or dry granulation converts fine powder into larger, free-flowing granules.
  3. Particle Size Enlargement: Spray drying, spheronization, or co-crystallization to produce spherical agglomerates.
  4. Moisture Control: Proper drying of powder and storage in controlled humidity conditions.
  5. Vibration/Agitation: Mechanical vibration in hoppers to overcome arching and ratholing.
  6. Flow Aids in Equipment: Using hopper designs with steeper angles, vibration devices, or air-assisted discharge.
  7. Surface Modification: Coating particles with hydrophobic materials to reduce moisture sensitivity.
  8. Optimization of Size Distribution: Adding fines to coarse particles (or vice versa) to achieve optimal packing.
💡 Exam Tip: Remember the order: Size > Shape > Moisture > Distribution are the most important factors. For improvement: Glidants > Granulation > Moisture control are most frequently used in practice.
Q.2 Viscometers | Thixotropy & Antithixotropy | Newtonian & Non-Newtonian Systems 16 Marks ▼
(a) Classification of Viscometers & Two Viscometers for Non-Newtonian Fluids [06 Marks]

Classification of Viscometers:

  1. Capillary Viscometers – Ostwald, Ubbelohde, Cannon-Fenske (based on Poiseuille's law)
  2. Rotational Viscometers –
    • Spindle type: Brookfield viscometer
    • Couette type (Concentric cylinder): Cup and bob viscometer
    • Cone and plate viscometer
  3. Falling Sphere/Body Viscometers – Hoeppler falling ball viscometer
  4. Orifice/Flow Cup Viscometers – Redwood, Saybolt, Ford cup viscometers
  5. Vibrational Viscometers – Ultrasonic viscometers

1. Brookfield Viscometer (Spindle Type Rotational Viscometer)

Principle: A spindle (rotor) of specific geometry is immersed in the test fluid and rotated at a defined speed (RPM). The viscous drag (torque) exerted by the fluid on the spindle is measured by a calibrated spring. The torque is directly related to the viscosity of the fluid.

┌────────────┐ │ Dial/ │ │ Display │ ├────────────┤ │ Motor & │ │ Spring │ ├──────┬─────┤ │ │ Shaft │ ┌────┴────┐ │ Spindle │ (Various shapes: disc, │ (Rotor) │ cylindrical, T-bar) └────┬────┘ │ ┌──────┴──────┐ │ Sample │ │ Beaker │ └─────────────┘

Working:

  • Select appropriate spindle (LV series for low viscosity, RV for medium, HV for high viscosity).
  • Immerse spindle to the marked depth in the sample.
  • Set RPM (0.3, 0.6, 1.5, 3, 6, 12, 30, 60 RPM).
  • The motor rotates the spindle; the torque produced is read on the dial/digital display.
  • Viscosity = Dial reading × Spindle factor × Speed factor (in cP).

Suitability for Non-Newtonian Fluids: By varying the RPM (shear rate), the apparent viscosity at different shear rates can be measured, allowing characterization of pseudoplastic, dilatant, and thixotropic behavior. A flow curve (shear stress vs. shear rate) can be constructed.

2. Cone and Plate Viscometer

Principle: A flat plate and a cone (with a very small angle, typically 0.5°–4°) are used. The sample is placed between the cone and plate. The cone is rotated, and the torque is measured. The geometry ensures uniform shear rate throughout the sample.

╱│╲ ╱ │ ╲ Cone (rotating) ╱ │ ╲ Angle θ = 0.5°-4° ╱______│______╲ ╲ │ ╱ Sample fills the gap ╲──────│──────╱ ────────────┴─────────── Flat Plate (stationary)

Shear Rate:

γ̇ = ω / tan θ

Where ω = angular velocity, θ = cone angle. Since θ is small, shear rate is nearly uniform across the gap.

Viscosity:

η = (3T) / (2πR³ω)

Where T = torque, R = cone radius.

Advantages for Non-Newtonian Fluids:

  • Uniform shear rate → accurate rheological data
  • Small sample volume required (~0.5–2 mL)
  • Excellent for thixotropic studies (hysteresis loop measurement)
  • Temperature control is easy (Peltier plate)
💡 Exam Tip: For Non-Newtonian fluids, rotational viscometers are preferred because they operate at defined shear rates. Capillary viscometers assume Newtonian behavior. Always mention that cone-and-plate gives uniform shear rate.
(b) Thixotropy and Antithixotropy [05 Marks]

Thixotropy:

Definition: Thixotropy is a time-dependent shear thinning property. When a thixotropic material is subjected to shear, its viscosity decreases over time. When shear is removed, the viscosity gradually recovers over time as the structure rebuilds.

Mechanism: Thixotropic materials possess a three-dimensional structural network (formed by weak bonds – van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonds) at rest. Under shear, this network is progressively broken down, reducing viscosity. Upon resting, the network slowly reforms.

Examples: Gels (bentonite, carbopol), certain paints, ketchup, some pharmaceutical gels and suspensions.

Antithixotropy (Rheopexy):

Definition: Antithixotropy is a time-dependent shear thickening property. Under constant shear, the viscosity increases over time. When shear is removed, viscosity gradually decreases back.

Mechanism: Under shear, particles align or form new structural interactions that increase resistance to flow. The structure becomes stronger with continued shear.

Examples: Concentrated starch suspensions, some PVC plastisols, certain colloidal iron oxide suspensions.

Thixotropic Curve (Hysteresis Loop):
Shear Stress (τ) │ │ ╭───╮ ↓ Up curve │ ╱ ╲ (Increasing shear rate) │ ╱ ╲ │ ╱ ╲ │ ╱ ╲ ↑ Down curve │ ╱ ╲ (Decreasing shear rate) │╱ ╲ └──────────────────── Shear Rate (γ̇) Thixotropic: Up curve is ABOVE Down curve Area of loop = Degree of thixotropy

Shear Stress (τ) │ │ ╲ ╱ │ ╲ ╱ ↓ Up curve (below) │ ╲ ╱ │ ╲ ╱ │ ╲ ╱ │ ╲ ╱ ↑ Down curve (above) │ ╰───╯ └──────────────────── Shear Rate (γ̇) Antithixotropic: Up curve is BELOW Down curve
Key Difference: Thixotropy = breakdown of structure on shear (loop area above); Antithixotropy = buildup of structure on shear (loop area below). Thixotropy is far more common in pharmaceuticals.
(c) Viscosity – Newtonian and Non-Newtonian Systems [05 Marks]

Definition of Viscosity:

Viscosity (η) is defined as the internal resistance of a fluid to flow. It is the ratio of shear stress to shear rate.

η = τ / γ̇

Where: τ = shear stress (force/area, dynes/cm² or Pa), γ̇ = shear rate (s−1)

Unit: Poise (P) or Pascal-second (Pa·s); 1 Pa·s = 10 P; commonly expressed in centipoise (cP); 1 cP = water viscosity at 20°C.

Newtonian Systems:

  • Obey Newton's law of viscosity: τ = η × γ̇
  • Viscosity is constant regardless of shear rate
  • Flow curve is a straight line passing through origin
  • Examples: Water, simple syrups, mineral oil, glycerin, true solutions

Non-Newtonian Systems:

Do NOT obey Newton's law. Viscosity changes with shear rate. Classified as:

Pseudoplastic (Shear thinning)Viscosity ↓ with ↑ shear rateCurve concave to τ axisPolymer solutions, cellulose derivatives, natural gums
Dilatant (Shear thickening)Viscosity ↑ with ↑ shear rateCurve concave to γ̇ axisConcentrated starch suspensions, wet sand
Plastic (Bingham body)Must overcome yield value (τy) before flowStraight line not through originToothpaste, ointments, ZnO suspension

Q.3 Compaction & Compression Theory | Kawakita & Heckle Equations | Flow Properties 16 Marks ▼
(a) Theory of Compaction & Compression [06 Marks]

Compression:

Definition: Compression is the reduction in volume of the powder bed by the application of external force (using upper and lower punches in a die). It involves the physical process of volume reduction.

Compaction:

Definition: Compaction is a broader term that includes both compression and the formation of bonds between particles to produce a coherent compact (tablet). It encompasses all the physical and chemical changes occurring during and after compression.

Key Distinction: Compression = volume reduction only. Compaction = volume reduction + bond formation + structural changes. All compaction involves compression, but not all compression is compaction.

Stages of Compaction:

  1. Stage I – Rearrangement (Filling & Packing): At low pressure, particles slide and rearrange to fill voids. Larger voids are eliminated first. No permanent deformation. Density increases moderately.
  2. Stage II – Deformation at Contact Points:
    • Elastic deformation: Particles deform but regain original shape when stress is removed (e.g., microcrystalline cellulose to some extent).
    • Plastic deformation: Particles undergo permanent deformation without fracture (e.g., microcrystalline cellulose is highly plastic).
    • Brittle fracture: Particles fragment into smaller pieces, creating new surfaces for bonding (e.g., lactose, dibasic calcium phosphate).
  3. Stage III – Bonding: At contact points, short-range forces act: van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonding, solid bridges (from melting/solidification), mechanical interlocking.
  4. Stage IV – Consolidation: Further pressure causes densification; the compact approaches its true density. Elastic recovery may occur upon pressure release (capping/lamination if excessive).

Bonding Mechanisms in Compaction:

  • Cold welding (Solid bridges): Clean particle surfaces come into atomic contact, forming metallic or molecular bonds.
  • Fusion bonding: Localized melting at contact points due to frictional heat, followed by solidification.
  • Intermolecular forces: Van der Waals, hydrogen bonding between surfaces.
  • Mechanical interlocking: Irregular particles interlock physically.
💡 Exam Tip: Always differentiate compression from compaction. Mention the 4 stages clearly. Give examples of plastic materials (MCC) vs brittle materials (lactose). Draw a pressure-density curve showing the stages.
(b) Kawakita and Heckle Equations [05 Marks]

1. Kawakita Equation:

Based on: Powder compression behavior at constant pressure, relating the degree of volume reduction to applied pressure.

C = (V0 − V) / V0 = aP / (1 + bP)

Or in linearized form:

P / C = (1 / ab) + (P / a)

Where:

  • C = Degree of volume reduction = (V0 − V) / V0
  • V0 = Initial volume of powder bed
  • V = Volume at pressure P
  • a = Kawakita constant = maximum compressibility (C at infinite pressure); represents the total volume reduction possible (0 < a < 1)
  • b = Kawakita constant = related to the pressure required for 50% compression; lower b means easier compression
  • P = Applied pressure

Use: Plot P/C vs P gives a straight line. Slope = 1/a, Intercept = 1/ab. Used to compare compressibility of different powders.

2. Heckel Equation:

Based on: The assumption that compression follows first-order kinetics with respect to the relative density (analogous to a chemical reaction). Describes the densification of powders by plastic deformation.

ln[1 / (1 − D)] = KP + A

Where:

  • D = Relative density of compact at pressure P = ρcompact / ρtrue
  • K = Heckel constant = slope = D0 / (Py)
  • Py = Yield pressure (inverse of K) – indicates the pressure at which yielding/plastic deformation begins. Lower Py = more plastic material.
  • A = Intercept = ln[1 / (1 − D0)] → related to initial packing (die filling and rearrangement)
  • D0 = Relative density at zero pressure (initial packing fraction)

Heckel Plot: ln[1/(1−D)] vs P gives a straight line for materials that undergo predominantly plastic deformation.

ln[1/(1-D)] │ / Plastic material │ / (steeper slope = lower Py) │ / │ / │ / ─── Brittle material │ / (shallower initial slope, │ / then steeper after fracture) │ / │ / │ / └──────────────────── P (Pressure)
Interpretation: • Steeper slope (higher K, lower Py) → More plastic material (e.g., MCC) • Shallower slope with deviation at low pressure → Brittle material, initial rearrangement followed by fracture (e.g., lactose) • Two linear regions indicate: first region = particle rearrangement, second = plastic deformation/fracture
📚 Short Revision Note: Kawakita = volume reduction approach (P/C vs P), good for loose powders. Heckel = density approach (ln[1/(1-D)] vs P), good for plastic deformation study. Py is the key parameter in Heckel – lower Py means more plastic.

Definition: The maximum angle between the surface of a cone-shaped powder pile and the horizontal plane. It indicates the frictional force between particles.

Determination: Powder is allowed to flow through a funnel onto a flat surface, forming a cone. Height (h) and radius (r) of the cone are measured.

Q.4 Properties of Colloids | Purification of Colloids | Applications 16 Marks ▼
(a) Properties of Colloids – Kinetic Properties [06 Marks]

General Properties of Colloids:

  1. Heterogeneity: Colloidal systems consist of two phases – dispersed phase and dispersion medium.
  2. Particle Size: Dispersed phase particles range from 1 nm to 1000 nm (0.001–1 μm).
  3. Tyndall Effect: Colloids scatter light when a beam is passed through them, producing a visible cone (Tyndall cone). True solutions do not show this.
  4. Brownian Motion: Random zigzag movement of colloidal particles due to bombardment by solvent molecules.
  5. Electrical Properties: Colloidal particles carry a charge (positive or negative), leading to electrophoresis and electroosmosis.
  6. Optical Properties: Colloids exhibit the Faraday-Tyndall effect; electron microscopy is needed to visualize particles.
  7. Colligative Properties: Osmotic pressure, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression are very small due to large particle size.
  8. Sedimentation: Very slow sedimentation due to small size; balanced by Brownian motion.
  9. Coagulation/Precipitation: Can be destabilized by adding electrolytes, heating, or oppositely charged colloids.
  10. Surface Area: Extremely large surface area to volume ratio, responsible for adsorption phenomena.

Kinetic Properties (Detailed):

1. Brownian Motion:

  • Discovered by Robert Brown (1827).
  • Colloidal particles exhibit continuous, random, zigzag motion due to unequal bombardment by molecules of the dispersion medium.
  • Einstein's equation for mean displacement:
x̄ = √(RTt / 3πηNAr)

Where: x̄ = mean displacement, R = gas constant, T = temperature, t = time, η = viscosity, NA = Avogadro's number, r = particle radius.

  • Smaller particles show more vigorous Brownian motion.
  • Higher temperature increases Brownian motion.
  • Greater viscosity decreases Brownian motion.

2. Diffusion:

  • Colloidal particles diffuse from high concentration to low concentration, though much slower than molecular diffusion.
  • Einstein's diffusion equation:
D = RT / (6πηrNA) = kT / (6πηr)

Where D = diffusion coefficient.

3. Sedimentation & Sedimentation Equilibrium:

  • Due to gravity, denser particles tend to settle. However, Brownian motion opposes settling.
  • At equilibrium, a concentration gradient is established.
  • Stokes' Law for sedimentation velocity:
v = 2r²(ρ − ρ0)g / (9η)

Where v = sedimentation velocity, r = radius, ρ = particle density, ρ0 = medium density, g = acceleration due to gravity.

4. Osmotic Pressure:

  • Van't Hoff equation modified for colloids:
π = cRT / M

Where c = concentration, M = molecular weight of colloidal particle. Since M is very large, π is very small.

(b) Purification Methods of Colloidal Solutions [05 Marks]

1. Dialysis:

Principle: A colloidal solution is placed in a bag made of a semipermeable membrane (cellophane, parchment paper, animal bladder) which allows only small molecules/ions to pass through but retains colloidal particles. The bag is suspended in pure water, and the impurities (electrolytes, small molecules) diffuse out.

Process: The external water is changed periodically to maintain concentration gradient. Takes several hours to days.

2. Electrodialysis:

Principle: Dialysis is accelerated by applying an electric field across the membrane. The charged impurity ions migrate faster toward the oppositely charged electrodes.

──────(+) Anode Cathode (−)────── │ │ Water │ Colloidal │ Water │ Solution │ │ ┌──────────┐ │ │ │ Dialysis │ │ └──│ Bag │──────┘ │ (colloid │ │ retained)│ └──────────┘ Ions migrate to electrodes → faster purification

3. Ultrafiltration:

Principle: Instead of relying on natural diffusion, pressure is applied to force the solvent and dissolved impurities through an ultrafilter membrane while retaining the colloidal particles.

Special filters with graded pore sizes are used. Can be combined with electrodialysis for faster results.

4. Centrifugation (Ultracentrifugation):

Principle: High centrifugal force (up to 100,000 times gravity) causes colloidal particles to sediment. The supernatant containing impurities is decanted, and the sediment is redispersed in pure solvent. Repeated to obtain pure colloid.

💡 Exam Tip: Dialysis is the most common method. Electrodialysis = dialysis + electricity. Ultrafiltration = dialysis + pressure. For exam, draw the dialysis setup diagram.
(c) Applications of Colloidal Solutions [05 Marks]

Pharmaceutical Applications:

  1. Drug Delivery Systems:
    • Liposomes (phospholipid vesicles) for targeted drug delivery
    • Niosomes, transfersomes for transdermal delivery
    • Polymeric nanoparticles for controlled release
  2. Parenteral Preparations: Colloidal solutions of silver (Argyrol), colloidal gold used as germicides and therapeutic agents.
  3. Ophthalmic Preparations: Colloidal silver proteins used as antiseptic eye drops.
  4. Natural Colloids in Medicine:
    • Blood plasma (colloidal solution of proteins) – plasma expander
    • Gelatin solutions used as plasma substitutes
    • Dextran solutions as blood volume expanders
  5. Diagnostic Applications:
    • Colloidal gold conjugates in immunochromatographic tests (pregnancy tests, rapid antigen tests)
  6. Dermatological Preparations: Calamine lotion (suspension of ZnO in colloidal form), colloidal sulfur for acne treatment.

Non-Pharmaceutical Applications:

  1. Food Industry: Milk, butter, mayonnaise, ice cream are all colloidal systems.
  2. Water Purification: Alum (Al2(SO4)3) causes coagulation of colloidal impurities.
  3. Industrial: Inks, paints, rubber latex, photographic emulsions.
  4. Analytical Chemistry: Colloidal gold used in electron microscopy staining.
Q.5 Second Order Kinetics | Determination of Reaction Order | First Order Kinetics 16 Marks ▼
(a) Second Order Kinetics – Derivation with Half Life and Shelf Life [06 Marks]

Definition:

In second order kinetics, the rate of reaction is directly proportional to the square of the concentration of a single reactant (or the product of concentrations of two reactants, each to the first power).

Rate Expression:

−d[A]/dt = k2[A]2

Where: k2 = second order rate constant (units: L mol−1 s−1 or M−1 s−1)

Derivation (for single reactant type: A → Products):

Starting with the rate law:

−d[A]/dt = k2[A]2

Separating variables:

−d[A]/[A]2 = k2 dt

Integrating from t = 0 to t = t, where [A]0 = initial concentration, [A] = concentration at time t:

∫[A]0[A] −d[A]/[A]2 = ∫0t k2 dt
[1/[A]][A]0[A] = k2[t]0t
1/[A] − 1/[A]0 = k2t

This is the integrated rate equation for second order kinetics.

Alternative form:

[A] = [A]0 / (1 + k2[A]0t)
Graphical Representation:

Plot of 1/[A] vs t gives a straight line with:

  • Slope = k2
  • Intercept = 1/[A]0

Half Life (t1/2):

At t1/2, [A] = [A]0/2. Substituting in the integrated equation:

1/([A]0/2) − 1/[A]0 = k2 × t1/2
2/[A]0 − 1/[A]0 = k2 × t1/2
t1/2 = 1 / (k2[A]0)
Important: Half life of a second order reaction is inversely proportional to initial concentration. Unlike first order, t1/2 is NOT constant – it increases as the reaction proceeds and concentration decreases.

Shelf Life (t90):

Shelf life is the time at which 90% of the drug remains (i.e., 10% degraded): [A] = 0.9[A]0

1/(0.9[A]0) − 1/[A]0 = k2 × t90
(1.111 − 1)/[A]0 = k2 × t90
t90 = 0.111 / (k2[A]0)

Or equivalently:

t90 = t1/2 / 9
(b) Determination of Reaction Order [05 Marks]

Methods to Determine Reaction Order:

1. Substitution Method (Trial and Error Method):

  • Substitute experimental concentration-time data into the integrated rate equations of zero, first, and second order.
  • The equation that gives a constant value of k for all time points indicates the order.
  • Simple but tedious; may give ambiguous results for fractional orders.

2. Graphical Method:

OrderPlotStraight line when
Zero[A] vs tSlope = −k0
Firstln[A] vs tSlope = −k1
Second1/[A] vs tSlope = k2

The plot that gives the best straight line (highest R²) indicates the reaction order.

3. Initial Rate Method (Differential Method):

  • Multiple experiments are performed with different initial concentrations [A]0.
  • The initial rate (slope of [A] vs t at t → 0) is determined for each.
  • From: Rate = k[A]0n, taking log: log(Rate) = log k + n × log[A]0
  • Plot log(Rate) vs log[A]0 → slope = order (n), intercept = log k.
n = log(Rate1/Rate2) / log([A]0,1/[A]0,2)

4. Half Life Method:

  • Perform experiments at different initial concentrations.
  • Determine t1/2 for each.
  • Relationship: t1/2 ∝ 1/[A]0(n−1)
  • Taking log: log(t1/2) = constant − (n−1) × log[A]0
  • Plot log(t1/2) vs log[A]0 → slope = −(n−1)

5. Ostwald's Isolation Method:

  • For reactions with multiple reactants, keep all but one at large excess (their concentrations remain essentially constant).
  • Determine the order with respect to each reactant separately.
  • Total order = sum of individual orders.
💡 Exam Tip: Graphical method is most commonly asked. Remember: Zero = [A] vs t, First = ln[A] vs t, Second = 1/[A] vs t. For initial rate method, always write the log equation.
(c) First Order Kinetics – Derivation with Half Life and Shelf Life [05 Marks]

Definition:

In first order kinetics, the rate of reaction is directly proportional to the first power of the concentration of a single reactant.

−d[A]/dt = k1[A]

Where: k1 = first order rate constant (units: s−1, min−1, hr−1, day−1)

Derivation:

Starting with the rate law:

−d[A]/dt = k1[A]

Separating variables:

−d[A]/[A] = k1 dt

Integrating from t = 0 to t = t:

−∫[A]0[A] d[A]/[A] = ∫0t k1 dt
−[ln[A]][A]0[A] = k1[t]0t
−(ln[A] − ln[A]0) = k1t
ln[A]0 − ln[A] = k1t

Or in various forms:

ln([A]0/[A]) = k1t
log([A]0/[A]) = k1t / 2.303
[A] = [A]0 × e−k1t
Graphical Representation:
  • Plot of ln[A] vs t → straight line, slope = −k1, intercept = ln[A]0
  • Plot of log[A] vs t → straight line, slope = −k1/2.303

Half Life (t1/2):

At t1/2, [A] = [A]0/2:

ln([A]0/([A]0/2)) = k1 × t1/2
ln(2) = k1 × t1/2
t1/2 = 0.693 / k1
Key Feature: Half life of a first order reaction is INDEPENDENT of initial concentration. This is the hallmark of first order kinetics and is of great importance in pharmacy (most drug degradation follows first order).

Shelf Life (t90):

At t90, [A] = 0.9[A]0:

ln([A]0/(0.9[A]0)) = k1 × t90
ln(1/0.9) = k1 × t90
ln(1.111) = k1 × t90
t90 = 0.1054 / k1

Or using log form:

t90 = 0.152 / k1 (when using log base 10: log(1/0.9) × 2.303/k1)
📚 Short Revision Note: First order: t1/2 = 0.693/k (independent of [A]0), t90 = 0.105/k. Second order: t1/2 = 1/(k[A]0) (depends on [A]0), t90 = 0.111/(k[A]0). Most drug degradation = first order.
Q.6 Emulsion & Theories | Flocculated vs Deflocculated | Surfactant & HLB 16 Marks ▼
(a) Emulsion – Definition & Theories of Emulsification [06 Marks]

Definition of Emulsion:

An emulsion is a thermodynamically unstable, biphasic system consisting of at least two immiscible liquids, one of which is dispersed as fine droplets (internal/dispersed phase) within the other (external/continuous phase), stabilized by an emulsifying agent.

Types: Oil-in-Water (O/W) – oil dispersed in water; Water-in-Oil (W/O) – water dispersed in oil.

Theories of Emulsification:

1. Surface Tension Theory (Reduced Surface Tension Theory):

  • Proposed by Worthington.
  • Emulsifying agents reduce the interfacial tension between the two immiscible liquids.
  • Lower interfacial tension → less energy required to break the dispersed phase into droplets → easier emulsification.
  • Limitation: Explains emulsion formation but not stability, as thermodynamically the system should still separate (emulsions are still unstable even with reduced tension).

2. Oriented Wedge Theory (Bancroft's Rule):

  • Proposed by Harkins.
  • The emulsifying agent forms a monolayer at the interface, with its molecules oriented such that the greater cross-sectional area faces the continuous phase.
  • If the emulsifier has a larger hydrophilic head (wedge shape with broad end in water) → O/W emulsion.
  • If the emulsifier has a larger lipophilic tail (broad end in oil) → W/O emulsion.
  • Bancroft's Rule: "The phase in which the emulsifier is more soluble becomes the continuous phase."
  • Limitation: Doesn't explain emulsions formed with ionic surfactants or solid particle emulsifiers.
O/W Emulsion: W/O Emulsion: Water (continuous) Oil (continuous) ═══════════════════ ═══════════════════ ○ ○ ○ ○ ○ ○ ○ ●●● ○ ← Water ○ ●●● ○ ← Oil ○ ○ ○ droplet ○ ○ ○ droplet ═══════════════════ ═══════════════════ Oil (dispersed) Water (dispersed) ●●● = Emulsifier (wedge shape oriented) Broad end always faces continuous phase

3. Interfacial Film Theory:

  • Proposed by Schulman and Cockbain.
  • The emulsifier forms a mechanically strong, coherent, and elastic film at the interface between the two liquids.
  • This film prevents coalescence of dispersed droplets by acting as a physical barrier.
  • Combined films (e.g., sodium cetyl sulfate + cholesterol) are stronger than single-component films.
  • This theory best explains emulsion stability.

4. Viscosity Theory:

  • The emulsifier increases the viscosity of the continuous phase.
  • Higher viscosity reduces the kinetic movement of dispersed droplets, slowing down coalescence.
  • Explains the stabilizing effect of gums (tragacanth, acacia) and polymers.

5. Electrical Double Layer Theory:

    li>Applicable to O/W emulsions stabilized by ionic emulsifiers.
  • The charged emulsifier creates an electrical double layer around each droplet, causing repulsion between droplets.
  • This electrostatic repulsion prevents coalescence.

(c) Surfactant & HLB Calculation Formulas [05 Marks]

Definition of Surfactant:

A surfactant (Surface Active Agent) is a substance that, when present at low concentration in a system, adsorbs at the interface between two immiscible phases (liquid-liquid, liquid-gas, or liquid-solid) and reduces the interfacial/surface tension. Surfactants possess both hydrophilic (water-loving) and lipophilic (oil-loving) regions in the same molecule (amphiphilic nature).

HLB (Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance):

Definition: HLB is a semi-empirical scale (developed by Griffin, 1949) that indicates the relative tendency of a surfactant to be hydrophilic or lipophilic. It ranges from 0 to 40 (typically 0–20 for most surfactants).

HLB Scale:

  • HLB 0–3: Antifoaming agents
  • HLB 3–6: W/O emulsifiers
  • HLB 7–9: Wetting agents
  • HLB 8–16: O/W emulsifiers
  • HLB 13–15: Detergents
  • HLB 15–18: Solubilizing agents

Formulas for HLB Calculation:

1. Griffin's Method – For Non-Ionic Surfactants (fatty acid esters):

HLB = 20 × (Mw of hydrophilic portion) / (Molecular weight of surfactant)
HLB = 20 × MH / M

2. Griffin's Method – For Fatty Alcohol Ethoxylates (AEOn):

HLB = (E% + P%) / 5

Where: E% = weight % of ethylene oxide, P% = weight % of polyhydric alcohol (glycerol, sorbitol) group.

If only ethylene oxide is present: HLB = E% / 5

3. Davies' Method – For All Surfactants (Group Number Method):

HLB = 7 + Σ(Hydrophilic group numbers) − Σ(Lipophilic group numbers)

Q.7 Suspension & Interfacial Properties | Emulsion Identification Tests | Accelerated Stability 16 Marks ▼
(a) Suspension & Interfacial Properties of Suspended Particles [06 Marks]

Definition of Suspension:

A suspension is a coarse dispersion of insoluble or sparingly soluble solid particles (typically 0.5–5 μm) in a liquid medium (usually aqueous), intended for oral, topical, or parenteral administration. The solid particles do not dissolve but remain suspended by physical or chemical means.

Interfacial Properties of Suspended Particles:

1. Surface Free Energy (Interfacial Tension):

  • When a solid particle is dispersed in a liquid, a solid-liquid interface is created.
  • The interfacial tension at this interface determines the degree of wetting.
  • High interfacial tension → poor wetting → particles tend to aggregate and float (hydrophobic particles).
  • Lowering interfacial tension (by wetting agents) improves dispersion.

2. Wetting:

  • Wetting is the displacement of air from the solid surface by the liquid.
  • Contact angle (θ) is the angle between the solid surface and the tangent to the liquid droplet at the point of contact.
  • θ < 90° = Good wetting (hydrophilic surface); θ > 90° = Poor wetting (hydrophobic surface).
  • Wetting agents (surfactants like polysorbate 80, SLS) reduce contact angle by adsorbing at the solid-liquid interface.

3. Electric Double Layer (Zeta Potential):

  • Particles in suspension acquire a surface charge (from ionization, ion adsorption, or lattice defects).
  • This charge attracts counterions from the medium, forming an electric double layer (Stern layer + diffuse layer).
  • The zeta potential (potential at the slipping plane) determines electrostatic repulsion between particles.
  • High |zeta potential| (> ±30 mV) → deflocculated (stable against aggregation but may form hard cake).
  • Low |zeta potential| (< ±20 mV) → flocculated (aggregates form but easily redispersible).

4. Adsorption at Interface:

  • Surfactants, polymers, and ions adsorb at the solid-liquid interface, modifying surface properties.
  • Adsorbed layers can provide steric stabilization (polymers like PVP, HPMC) or electrostatic stabilization (ionic surfactants).

5. Solid-Liquid Interfacial Phenomena in Practice:

  • Floating tendency: Hydrophobic particles entrapped air bubbles → float to surface. Wetting agents solve this.
  • Particle-particle adhesion: Due to van der Waals forces at contact points, enhanced by capillary forces from adsorbed moisture films.
  • Caking: In deflocculated suspensions, settled particles form a compact mass with strong interparticle bonds, making redispersion difficult.
(b) Identification Tests for Emulsion [05 Marks]

Tests to Identify O/W and W/O Emulsions:

1. Dye Solubility Test (Dilution Test):

  • Add a water-soluble dye (e.g., amaranth, methylene blue) to the emulsion.
  • If the dye disperses throughout → O/W emulsion (water is continuous phase).
  • If the dye remains as discrete droplets → W/O emulsion (water is dispersed phase).
  • Alternatively, use an oil-soluble dye (Sudan III, Oil Red O): disperses throughout in W/O, remains as droplets in O/W.

2. Electrical Conductivity Test:

  • Insert electrodes connected to a conductivity meter/bulb into the emulsion.
  • High conductivity (bulb glows) → O/W emulsion (water is continuous and conducts electricity).
  • Low/no conductivity (bulb does not glow) → W/O emulsion (oil is continuous and non-conductive).

3. Filter Paper Test:

  • Place a drop of emulsion on a piece of filter paper.
  • If the drop spreads quickly → O/W emulsion (external water phase is absorbed by the hydrophilic filter paper).
  • If the drop does not spread → W/O emulsion (external oil phase is not absorbed).

4. Cobalt Chloride Test:

  • Place a drop of emulsion on cobalt chloride-impregnated filter paper (blue when dry, pink when wet).
  • If the paper turns pink → O/W emulsion (water in continuous phase reaches the paper).
  • If the paper remains blue → W/O emulsion.

5. Fluorescence Test:

  • Examine the emulsion under UV light.
  • Many oils exhibit fluorescence. If the entire field fluoresces → W/O; if only discrete droplets fluoresce → O/W.

6. Dilution Test:

  • Try to dilute the emulsion with water. If it mixes freely → O/W. If it separates → W/O.
  • Try to dilute with oil. If it mixes freely → W/O. If it separates → O/W.
💡 Exam Tip: Dye test, conductivity test, and filter paper test are the three most important. Always write the expected result for both O/W and W/O types for each test.
(c) Accelerated Stability Testing in Expiration Dating [05 Marks]

Definition:

Accelerated stability testing involves subjecting the pharmaceutical product to elevated stress conditions (temperature, humidity, light) for a shorter duration to predict its shelf life under normal storage conditions. It is based on the Arrhenius equation, which relates reaction rate to temperature.

Principle – Arrhenius Equation:

k = A × e−Ea/RT

In linearized form:

ln k = ln A − Ea/(RT)
log k = log A − Ea/(2.303RT)

Where:

  • k = rate constant at temperature T
  • A = frequency factor (pre-exponential factor)
  • Ea = activation energy (J/mol or cal/mol)
  • R = gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)
  • T = absolute temperature (K)

Procedure:

  1. Sample Preparation: Prepare multiple samples of the dosage form in their final packaging.
  2. Storage Conditions: Store at elevated temperatures (e.g., 40°C, 50°C, 60°C, 70°C) with controlled humidity (e.g., 75% RH).
  3. ICH Guidelines for Storage Conditions:

  1. Sampling & Analysis: Withdraw samples at regular time intervals and assay the drug content.
  2. Determination of k at each temperature: Using the appropriate kinetic model (usually first order), calculate k at each temperature.
  3. Arrhenius Plot: Plot log k vs 1/T (K−1). This gives a straight line.

  1. Extrapolation: Extrapolate the line to room temperature (25°C = 298 K) to obtain k25.
  2. Calculation of Shelf Life: Using k25, calculate t90 (time for 10% degradation):
t90 = 0.1054 / k25 (for first order)
t90 = 0.1[A]0 / k25 (for zero order)

Objectives of Accelerated Stability Testing:

  • To predict the shelf life (expiration date) of the product without waiting for real-time data.
  • To identify the degradation pathway and products.
  • To determine the effect of packaging on stability.
  • To establish storage conditions and labeling recommendations.
  • To provide regulatory submission data (ICH, FDA, CDSCO guidelines).
Important Notes: • Accelerated data is used for provisional expiry dates; confirmatory long-term data is still required. • The Arrhenius equation is valid only when the degradation mechanism remains the same at all temperatures. • Products showing phase changes (melting, crystallization) at elevated temperatures cannot be reliably predicted by Arrhenius.
